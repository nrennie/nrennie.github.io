Education

PhD Statistics and Operational Research, Lancaster University

I'm currently undertaking a PhD at STOR-i CDT in Lancaster, due to finish in September 2021. My PhD is entitled "Data-Driven Alerts in Revenue Management", and is supervised by Catherine Cleophas (Christian-Albrechts Universität zu Kiel), Adam Sykulski (Lancaster University), and Florian Dost (University of Manchester). Revenue management maximizes revenue by optimizing prices or the offered set of fare classes based on forecasted demand. Data-driven alerts rely on pattern recognition and are already common in the domain of credit card fraud detection. A similar principle could apply in airline revenue management, detecting booking behaviour that deviates significantly from the automated forecasts' predictions. The resulting alerts could highlight relevant targets for interventions to improve the automated system. The objective of the project is to develop methods to highlight deviations between real-world observations and system expectations to assist analysts in targeting manual interventions to the quasi-automated revenue management systems.

In August 2020, I was awarded the Nick Smith Prize for Research Excellence.

PhD Research

My PhD is entitled "Data-driven Alerts in Revenue Management", and seeks to analyse and support the role of analysts within revenue management (RM) systems. RM solves an optimises a set of offers for perishable products, with the objective being to maximise revenue. This process relies heavily on accurate forecasts of demand. Unexpected events can cause outlier demand, which RM systems need to respond to in order to remian optimal. In this case, most RM systems allow analysts to manually override the forecast and make an adjustment. However, outlier demand can be difficult to spot by eye since humans are fallible, and often see patterns that don't exist. My PhD research explores statistical methods for automatically identifying outlier demand, and therefore situations, which would benefit from analyst adjustment.

Air Pollution Analysis

During my PhD, my research focused on developing methods to identify outlier demand in booking patterns for trains in railway networks. To demonstrate that these statistical methods are also applicable in other areas, I started to analyse air pollution data across the United Kingdom. The aim is to identify days with abnormal pollution levels. The data comes from Defra's Automatic Urban and Rural Network (AURN), which reports the level of nitrogen dioxide in the air every hour at 187 different locations. The data can be downloaded from uk-air.defra.gov.uk/data/data_selector_service . For this analysis, I considered data from 2014 to 2018.

AURN stations


I started by considering the data in a single station - Aberdeen. The nitrogen dioxide levels follow a similar pattern each day, with higher levels around rush hour in the morning and evening.

Functional analysis treats the pollution pattern for each day as an observation of a function over time. This allows us to compare the differences between patterns on different days, without complications from the varying pollution levels throughout each day.

Nitrogen dioxide levels in Aberdeen


The higher levels of pollution around 8am and 6pm are most likely caused by people travelling to work. This raises the question of whether pollution levels are lower on weekends, when there are generally fewer people travelling to work. I considered whether there were different pollution patterns on different days of the week, specifically weekdays vs. weekends.

To test this formally, a functional ANOVA test can be applied. This tests whether the average pollution pattern on a weekday is significantly different from the avergage pollution pattern on a weekend. The test returns the probability that, if the two pollution patterns were equal, the observed patterns would be this different. For this analysis, the probability was 0. Therefore, we can conclude there is a significant difference in the pollution levels on weekdays compared to weekends. We will analyse weekdays and weekends separately.

Nitrogen dioxide levels weekdays vs. weekends


To describe how normal (or abnormal) the pollution pattern for each day is, we calculate the functional depth. A depth measurement attributes a sensible ordering to observations, such that observations which are close to average have higher depth and those far from the average have lower depth. Days which have very low depth can be classified as outliers. Functional depth takes into account the abnormality both in the magnitude (for example, overall higher pollution levels throughout the day), and shape (for example, pollution levels peak earlier in the day than normal) of the pollution pattern.

Functional depths for weekends


A threshold is then calculated for the functional depths. If the functional depth for a given day is below that threshold, the day is classified as an outlier.

Outliers detected in nitrogen dioxide levels


The days which were detected as outliers in this analysis occur around early November, late December, and early January. Although this may suggest there are seasonal trends with generally higher pollution levels in winter, further investigation found this not to be the case. One possible explanation for why these dates were classified as having outlying levels of nitrogen dioxide, is fireworks. Fireworks are commonly set off on Bonfire Night, around Christmas, and New Year's Eve. Fireworks have been shown to contribute to elevated levels of gaseous pollutants, including nitrogen dioxide.

UK 2019 General Election Analysis

The 2019 United Kingdom general election was held on Thursday, 12 December 2019. The Conservative Party won a majority with 365 of the 650 seats. Here, we'll take a look at some interesting findings in the election data. The data can be downloaded from commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-8749 .

Examining the Gender Imbalance

Let's start by looking at all candidates who stood for election: what percentage were male, female, and non-binary?

Gender of candidates

Gender of candidates by party

A total of 3320 candidates stood for election, where 66.1% were mmale, 33.8% were female, and just 0.1% were non-binary. Of all main parties, only Labour had a higher number of female candidates than male. The rest were overwhelmingly male. Almost none of non-binary candidates were represented by any of the main parties. In fact, the percentage of non-binary candidates who represented alternative parties (or were independent), was higher than the percentage of male or female.

Now let's look at the 650 candidates who won.

Gender of elected candidates

Gender of elected candidates by party

The percentages of elected male, female, and non-binary cadidates mostly reflcets the proportion of candidates who stood for each party. There are two exceptions: the Liberal Democrat Party and the Green Party. In both cases, a far higher percentage of female candidates won, than stood for election. Alongside the Labour party, these were the only mainstream parties who elected more female than male MPs. Although this may indicate that there is a trend among left-leaning parties to elect more female MPs, the number of seats won by both the Liberal Democrat and Green parties was very small, so we can't be sure this is a pattern.

Further analysis on the alternative outcomes had people voted differently, and a model predicting a candidate's chances of winning, will be available soon.
